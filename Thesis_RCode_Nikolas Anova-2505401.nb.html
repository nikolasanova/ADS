bic_est <- as.data.frame(boot_mse_cart1_bic$tables$J)

bic_est

the AIC model produced population estimations that had higher normalized standard error than the BIC model. Therefore, based on the normalized standard error, the BIC model produces a better estimation of the human trafficking population than the AIC model.

Reasonableness of Population Estimation

Reasonableness of Population Estimation can be seen from Ratio of Estimated (NHat) and Observed (nobs) population. If the estimated population does not explode in number compared to the observed population, it can be said that the estimated population is still reasonable.

# Calculate ratio between estimated and observed victims (reasonableness population)
# AIC version
aic_est$ratio <- round(aic_est$Nhat/aic_est$nobs,1)
aic_est
# BIC version
bic_est$ratio <- round(bic_est$Nhat/bic_est$nobs,1)
bic_est

The ratio values of the two models (AIC and BIC) between the estimated and observed populations are still reasonable and do not differ much. So the population estimates of both models are still reasonable.

Recapitulation

Based on the comparison of the three criteria above, the BIC model is better than the AIC model. So, the BIC version model (from cart imputation) is chosen as the best model.

Conclusion

From the seven multiple imputation methods used to impute data on Human Trafficking Victims in the Netherlands 2016 - 2019, the classification and regression trees (CART) method produces the best Multiple System Estimation (MSE) model compared to other imputation methods.

Different imputation methods produce quite varied MSE model scores. This shows that, if there is a problem of missing data in the application of MSE, the choice of imputation method is proven to affect the quality of the output from MSE.

When compared by version, the BIC model from the CART method is better than its the AIC version. This can be seen from the complexity of the model, standard error and reasonableness of the population estimation. The BIC model is better on the criteria of model complexity and standard error.
